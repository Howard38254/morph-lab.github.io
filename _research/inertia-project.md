## Overview

This research investigates how changes in mass distribution and inertia influence aircraft maneuverability and flight dynamics. 
Traditional aircraft rely primarily on aerodynamic forces for control, while in nature birds actively modify their inertia through 
wing morphing. This project aims to isolate and quantify the role of shifting inertia and center of mass in flight by developing a 
morphing-wing UAV while also incorporating an internal shifting center of mass mechanism. The two separate approaches can isolate the 
contribution of center of mass and inertia to flight dynamics. This work hopes to see whether inertia can be used as an additional 
control mechanism to enhance agility and stability in flight.


## Methods
A baseline UAV consisting of standard wing with internal center of mass shifting and an additional UAV with morphing wing and shifting 
center of mass will be primarily used for experimentation. The two UAV configurations will be developed and evaluated using XFLR5 
program. Moment coefficients, lift, and stability of each UAV will be gathered for initial analysis. To validate simulation and modeling 
results, both UAVS will be constructed to best fit that of the simulation and tested through real flight. Controlled testing under 
constant conditions will be done to gather characteristic of the UAVS. Free flight testing will then gather flight path and key 
performance metrics.


## Key Findings

The working hypothesis is that deliberate changes in moment of inertia and center of mass can measurably alter UAV maneuverability 
and stability, and that these variation may provide a viable supplemental control mechanism.
